## Hypertension (High Blood Pressure)

### Overview
Hypertension is defined as systolic blood pressure ≥130 mmHg or diastolic blood pressure ≥80 mmHg (AHA/ACC 2017 guidelines). It affects approximately 1.28 billion adults worldwide and is a leading risk factor for heart disease, stroke, and kidney failure.

### Classification
- Normal: <120/<80 mmHg
- Elevated: 120-129/<80 mmHg
- Stage 1 Hypertension: 130-139/80-89 mmHg
- Stage 2 Hypertension: ≥140/≥90 mmHg
- Hypertensive Crisis: >180/>120 mmHg (requires immediate care)

### First-Line Treatment
1. Lifestyle modifications (all stages)
   - DASH diet (Dietary Approaches to Stop Hypertension)
   - Reduce sodium intake to <2,300 mg/day (ideally <1,500 mg/day)
   - Regular aerobic exercise: 150 minutes/week moderate intensity
   - Weight loss: 1 mmHg reduction per kg of weight lost
   - Limit alcohol consumption
   - Smoking cessation

2. Pharmacotherapy (Stage 1+ with CVD risk, or Stage 2)
   - ACE inhibitors (e.g., lisinopril, enalapril)
   - ARBs (e.g., losartan, valsartan)
   - Thiazide diuretics (e.g., hydrochlorothiazide, chlorthalidone)
   - Calcium channel blockers (e.g., amlodipine, nifedipine)

## Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus

### Diagnostic Criteria (ADA 2024)
- Fasting plasma glucose ≥126 mg/dL (7.0 mmol/L)
- 2-hour plasma glucose ≥200 mg/dL (11.1 mmol/L) during OGTT
- HbA1c ≥6.5% (48 mmol/mol)
- Random plasma glucose ≥200 mg/dL with symptoms

### Management Goals
- HbA1c target: <7.0% for most adults
- Blood pressure: <130/80 mmHg
- LDL cholesterol: <70 mg/dL for high-risk patients

### Medication Classes
1. Metformin — first-line, reduces hepatic glucose production
2. GLP-1 receptor agonists (semaglutide, liraglutide) — weight loss benefit, CV protection
3. SGLT2 inhibitors (empagliflozin, dapagliflozin) — renal and CV protection
4. DPP-4 inhibitors (sitagliptin, saxagliptin) — weight neutral
5. Insulin — when glycemic targets not met

## Common Drug Interactions

### Warfarin Interactions
- **Increases INR (bleeding risk)**: amoxicillin, metronidazole, fluconazole, NSAIDs, aspirin
- **Decreases INR (clotting risk)**: rifampin, carbamazepine, St. John's Wort
- Monitor INR frequently when starting/stopping any medication

### ACE Inhibitor Cautions
- Avoid with: potassium-sparing diuretics (hyperkalemia risk), NSAIDs (reduced efficacy + renal risk)
- Contraindicated in pregnancy (teratogenic)
- Monitor: serum creatinine, potassium, blood pressure

## Cardiovascular Risk Assessment

### Framingham Risk Score Factors
- Age and sex
- Total cholesterol and HDL cholesterol
- Systolic blood pressure and treatment status
- Smoking status
- Diabetes

### Primary Prevention Targets
- Statin therapy for 10-year ASCVD risk ≥7.5%
- Aspirin: NOT routinely recommended for primary prevention (2022 USPSTF)
- BP target: <130/80 mmHg

## Emergency Warning Signs

⚠️ **Call 911 / Emergency Services Immediately For:**
- Chest pain or pressure lasting >15 minutes
- Sudden severe headache ("thunderclap")
- Sudden weakness/numbness on one side of body
- Sudden vision loss or double vision
- Difficulty speaking or understanding speech (stroke symptoms)
- Blood pressure >180/120 mmHg with symptoms
- Signs of anaphylaxis (throat swelling, severe hives, difficulty breathing)
- Altered mental status

## Medication Safety: Common Side Effects

### Statins (e.g., atorvastatin, rosuvastatin)
- Myalgia (muscle pain) — 5-10% of patients
- Rare: rhabdomyolysis (severe muscle breakdown)
- Liver enzyme elevation (monitor ALT/AST)
- Drug interactions: CYP3A4 inhibitors increase statin levels

### Metformin
- GI side effects (nausea, diarrhea) — take with food
- Rare: lactic acidosis (hold before contrast dye procedures)
- Vitamin B12 deficiency with long-term use
- Safe in CKD stages 1-3 (eGFR ≥30 mL/min)

### NSAIDs (Ibuprofen, Naproxen)
- GI ulceration and bleeding risk (use with PPI if at risk)
- Cardiovascular risk (especially high-dose, long-term)
- Acute kidney injury (especially in volume-depleted patients)
- Avoid in heart failure, CKD, and third trimester of pregnancy
